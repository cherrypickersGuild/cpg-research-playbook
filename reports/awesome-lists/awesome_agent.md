## Synthesis

The open-source side of this list traces the arc of the agent field from 2023's "autonomous goal-chaining" demos (AutoGPT, BabyAGI, JARVIS, Voyager) through 2023–24's multi-agent orchestration frameworks (AutoGen, CrewAI, MetaGPT, CAMEL, ChatDev, Langroid) into today's specialized coding agents (Aider, SWE-agent, Open Interpreter, OpenHands/OpenDevin). A striking pattern across nearly every entry checked is churn: projects get renamed (MemGPT → Letta, OpenDevin → OpenHands, Fixie → Ultravox), archived (GPT Engineer, the classic BabyAGI), explicitly deprecated by their own maintainers (AutoGen "maintenance mode," GPT Pilot "not maintained," SWE-agent superseded by mini-swe-agent), or acquired into larger platforms (Continue → Cursor, Codegen → ClickUp). On the closed-source side, the trend is convergence toward full-lifecycle and workflow-embedded agents: coding assistants (Cursor, GitHub Copilot, GitLab Duo, Factory, Devin) now compete on how much of the SDLC they automate end-to-end, while business-facing agent platforms (Zapier Central/Agents, Gumloop, Relevance AI, Taskade, Lindy) compete on no-code accessibility and governance for non-engineers. For an AX education program, the clearest takeaway is that "which agent tool" matters less than "which capability class" (autonomous coding, multi-agent orchestration, memory/context, workflow automation, or voice/action agents) — and that freshness/maintenance status must be checked at time of adoption, since this space turns over fast enough that even 18-month-old flagship projects are frequently archived, renamed, or folded into acquirers.
